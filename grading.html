Assignment descriptions

Participation — demo notebooks + warm-ups

This grade has two components that are tracked separately and combined.

Demo notebooks: Every week you follow along in a Jupyter notebook, recording all code and exercises for that week. Notebooks are due by end of day Monday and graded for participation: 1 point for submitting, 1 point for having all exercises completed. There are 15 weeks of class — the lowest 2 notebook grades are dropped.

Warm-up problems: A short coding problem is posed at the start of each lecture via TopHat. Warm-ups are worth 1 point each. There are 41 lectures this semester — the lowest 8 warm-up scores are dropped, which means you have roughly one free pass per month without any grade penalty. Completing warm-ups on time rewards showing up ready to work; there is no penalty for an incorrect answer, only for not responding.

Coding assignments

Five take-home assignments throughout the semester designed to build and solidify your Python skills. Graduate students have additional exercises on each assignment. Submitted via Brightspace in weeks 1–5 and via GitHub Classroom from Week 6 onward.

Labs

The Monday afternoon lab period (1:25–4:15pm) is the core of the course. Each week you work through an applied problem using that week's tools and real Earth science data. Labs are due the following Sunday at 11:59pm. There are 11 graded labs this semester — Week 1 is an ungraded orientation; Labor Day and Thanksgiving reduce the total from 15.

Proposal workshop

In preparation for your final project, you will participate in a peer feedback workshop and submit a project proposal. You'll pitch your Final Coding Project theme and methods to peers and receive written feedback. Your proposal must be submitted before arriving at the Week 13 lab. Attendance and substantive written feedback on your peers' work are required for full credit.

Final coding project

Create a Scientific Demo Notebook that explores a real Earth science question through data analysis or numerical modeling. Your notebook should tell a story — walking a reader through your scientific question, showing your analysis step-by-step, and discussing what you learned. It should combine scientific narrative, working code, results, and interpretation. Graduate students: your project should use data relevant to your research. Submitted via GitHub Classroom. Due date announced during Week 13.

Python package presentation Graduate only

Each graduate student presents a Python package not covered in class (10–15 minutes) at the start of a lab period in weeks 7–11. The package should be relevant to your research area. Sign up for a slot in Week 6.

Git assignment Graduate only

A short practical assignment covering branching, merging, and pull requests — skills that translate directly to collaborative research workflows. Due Week 15. Details distributed in Week 6.

Late work

10% deduction per day for late submissions. It's unfair to your peers if you receive more time than they do.

In-class notebooks cannot be submitted later than midnight the Monday after they are due. Coding assignments cannot be submitted later than midnight one week after they are due. The proposal workshop and final project cannot be submitted late.

If you need an extension, contact Dr. Morey before the deadline — not after.
